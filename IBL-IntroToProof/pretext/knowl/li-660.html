Item 20.

Write “Let \(x=\dots\text{,}\)” not “Let \(\dots=x\text{.}\)” When you have an existing expression, say \(a^{2}\text{,}\) and you want to give it a new, simpler name like \(b\text{,}\) you should write “Let \(b=a^{2}\text{,}\)” which means, “Let the new symbol \(b\) mean \(a^{2}\text{.}\)” This convention makes it clear to the reader that \(b\) is the brand-new symbol and \(a^{2}\) is the old expression he/she already understands. If you were to write it backwards, saying “Let \(a^{2}=b\text{,}\)” then your startled reader would ask, “What if \(a^{2}\neq b\text{?}\)”
