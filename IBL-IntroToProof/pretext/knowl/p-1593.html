But what if the sets are infinite? For example, consider the set of natural numbers \(\mathbb{N}\) and the set of even natural numbers \(2\mathbb{N}\coloneqq \{2n\mid n\in \mathbb{N}\}\text{.}\) Clearly, \(2\mathbb{N}\) is a proper subset of \(\mathbb{N}\text{.}\) Moreover, both sets are infinite. In this case, you might be thinking that \(\mathbb{N}\) is “larger than” \(2\mathbb{N}\text{.}\) However, it turns out that there is a one-to-one correspondence between these two sets. In particular, consider the function \(f:\mathbb{N}\to 2\mathbb{N}\) defined via \(f(n)=2n\text{.}\) It is easily verified that \(f\) is both injective and surjective. In this case, mathematics has determined that the right viewpoint is that \(\mathbb{N}\) and \(2\mathbb{N}\) do have the same “size”. However, it is clear that “size” is a bit too imprecise when it comes to infinite sets. We need something more rigorous.
