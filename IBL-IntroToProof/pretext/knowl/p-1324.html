In mathematics, we say that an expression is well defined (or unambiguous) if its definition yields a unique interpretation. Otherwise, we say that the expression is not well defined (or is ambiguous). For example, if \(a,b,c\in\mathbb{R}\text{,}\) then the expression \(abc\) is well defined since it does not matter if we interpret this as \((ab)c\) or \(a(bc)\) since the real numbers are associative under multiplication. This issue was lurking behind the scenes in the statement of Theorem 7.97. In particular, the expressions
\begin{equation*} [a_1]_n+[a_2]_n+\cdots+ [a_k]_n \end{equation*}
and
\begin{equation*} [a_1]_n [a_2]_n \cdots [a_k]_n \end{equation*}
are well defined in \(\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}\) in light of Theorems 7.95 Item b and Theorem 7.96 Item b.
