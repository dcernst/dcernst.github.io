It remains to show that \(q\) and \(r\) are unique. Suppose \(q_1,q_2, r_1,r_2\in\mathbb{Z}\) such that \(n=dq_1+r_1\) and \(n=dq_2+r_2\) and \(0\leq r_1,r_2\lt d\text{.}\) Without loss of generality, suppose \(r_2\geq r_1\text{,}\) so that \(0\leq r_2-r_1\lt d\text{.}\) Since \(dq_1+r_1=dq_2+r_2\text{,}\) we see that \(r_2-r_1=d(q_1-q_2)\text{.}\) But then \(d\) divides \(r_2-r_1\text{.}\) If \(r_2-r_1>0\text{,}\) then by TheoremÂ 2.56, it must be the case that \(r_2-r_1\geq d\text{.}\) However, we know \(0\leq r_2-r_1\lt d\text{,}\) and so we must have \(r_2-r_1=0\text{.}\) Therefore, \(r_1=r_2\text{,}\) which in turn implies \(q_1=q_2\text{.}\) We have shown that \(q\) and \(r\) are unique.
