The next result is a special case of a theorem known as Bézout’s Lemma (or Bézout’s Identity). Ultimately, we will need this theorem to prove Euclid’s Lemma (Theorem 6.15), which we then use to prove uniqueness for the Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic (Theorem 6.17). To prove our special case of Bézout’s Lemma, consider the set \(S\coloneqq \{ps+at> 0\mid s,t\in \mathbb{Z}\}\text{.}\) First, observe that \(p\in S\) (choose \(s=1\) and \(t=0\)). It follows that \(S\) is nonempty. By the Well-Ordering Principle (Theorem 4.36), \(S\) contains a least element, say \(d\text{.}\) This implies that there exists \(s_1,t_1\in \mathbb{Z}\) such that \(d=ps_1+at_1\text{.}\) Our goal is to show that \(d=1\text{.}\) Now, choose \(m\in S\text{.}\) Then there exists \(s_2,t_2\in \mathbb{Z}\) such that \(m=ps_2+at_2\text{.}\) By the definition of \(d\text{,}\) we know \(d\leq m\text{.}\) By the Division Algorithm, there exists unique \(q,r\in \mathbb{N}\cup\{0\}\) such that \(m=qd+r\) with \(0\leq r \lt d\text{.}\) Now, solve for \(r\) and then replace \(m\) and \(d\) with \(ps_1+at_1\) and \(ps_2+at_2\text{,}\) respectively. You should end up with an expression for \(r\) involving \(p, a, s_1, s_2, t_1\text{,}\) and \(t_2\text{.}\) Next, rearrange this expression to obtain \(r\) as a linear combination of \(p\) and \(a\) (i.e., a sum of a multiple of \(p\) and a multiple of \(a\)). What does the minimality of \(d\) imply about \(r\text{?}\) You should be able to conclude that \(m\) is a multiple of \(d\text{.}\) That is, every element of \(S\) is a multiple of \(d\text{.}\) However, recall that \(p\in S\text{,}\) \(p\) is prime, and \(p\) and \(a\) are relatively prime. What can you conclude about \(d\text{?}\)
