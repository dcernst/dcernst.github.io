According to Theorem 9.58 and Corollary 9.65, \(\mathbb{R}\) and \(\mathcal{P}(\mathbb{N})\) are both uncountable. In fact, \(\card(\mathcal{P}(\mathbb{N}))=\card(\mathbb{R})\text{,}\) which we state without proof. However, it turns out that the two uncountable sets may or may not have the same cardinality. Perhaps surprisingly, there are sets that are even “bigger” than the set of real numbers. The next theorem is named after Georg Cantor, who first stated and proved it at the end of the 19th century. The theorem states that given any set, we can always increase the cardinality by considering its power set. That is, if \(A\) is a set, \(\mathcal{P}(A)\) has strictly greater cardinality than \(A\) itself. For finite sets, Cantor’s theorem follows from Theorems 4.12 and Theorem 4.13 (both of which we proved via induction). Perhaps much more surprising is that Cantor discovered an elegant argument that is applicable to any set, whether finite or infinite. To prove Cantor’s Theorem, first exhibit an injective function from \(A\) to \(\mathcal{P}(A)\text{.}\) This proves that \(\card(A)\leq \card(\mathcal{P}(A))\text{.}\) To show that \(\card(A)\lt \card(\mathcal{P}(A))\text{,}\) try a proof by contradiction. That is, assume there exists a bijective function \(f:A\to\mathcal{P}(A))\text{.}\) Derive a contradiction by considering the set \(B=\{x\in A\mid x\notin f(x)\}\text{.}\)
