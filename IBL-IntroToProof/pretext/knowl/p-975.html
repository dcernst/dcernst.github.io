The next result makes up half of the Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic. We provide a substantial hint for its proof. Let \(S\) be the set of natural numbers for which the theorem fails. For sake of a contradiction, assume \(S\neq \emptyset\text{.}\) By the Well-Ordering Principle (TheoremÂ 4.36), \(S\) contains a least element, say \(n\text{.}\) Then \(n\) cannot be prime since this would satisfy the theorem. So, it must be the case that \(n\) has a divisor other than 1 and itself. This implies that there exists natural numbers \(a\) and \(b\) greater than 1 such that \(n=ab\text{.}\) Since \(n\) was our smallest counterexample, what can you conclude about both \(a\) and \(b\text{?}\) Use this information to derive a contradiction for \(n\text{.}\)
