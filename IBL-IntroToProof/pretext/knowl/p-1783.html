Use appropriate quantifiers (once). When you introduce a variable \(x\in S\text{,}\) it must be clear to your reader whether you mean “for all \(x\in S\)” or just “for some \(x\in S\text{.}\)” If you just say something like “\(y=x^2\) where \(x\in S\text{,}\)” the word “where” does not indicate whether you mean “for all” or “some”. Phrases indicating the quantifier “for all” include “Let \(x\in S\)”; “for all \(x\in S\)”; “for every \(x\in S\)”; “for each \(x\in S\)”; etc. Phrases indicating the quantifier “some” or “there exists” include “for some \(x\in S\)”; “there exists an \(x\in S\)”; “for a suitable choice of \(x\in S\)”; etc. Once you have said “Let \(x\in S\text{,}\)” the letter \(x\) has its meaning defined. You do not need to say “for all \(x\in S\)” again, and you definitely should not say “let \(x\in S\)” again.
